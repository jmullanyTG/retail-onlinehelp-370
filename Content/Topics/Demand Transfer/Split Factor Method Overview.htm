Split Factor Method Overview

Split factors are a method of calculating fulfillment demand.

A split factor is simply the percentage of a location’s historical sales that were fulfilled at a given location. For example, if Store A fulfilled 100% of its past sales (meaning all items bought from Store A were fulfilled by Store A), the split factor for Store A sales would be Store A = 100%.

If Store A only fulfilled 90% of its past sales and the remaining 10% of sales was fulfilled at Store B, the split factors for Store A's sales would be: Store A = 90%, Store B = 10%.

Split factors must be calculated for all locations in which an item is sold. This gives Demand Management a complete history of where sales at each location were fulfilled. This fulfillment history, captured as split factors, can then be applied to each location's sales forecasts. The result is a forecast for future fulfillment demand at each location.

In Store A's case, if 10% of its sales are expected to be fulfilled by Store B, then replenishment orders for both stores need to be modified accordingly. Otherwise, Store A may end up being overstocked while Store B may experience "unexpected" stockouts after fulfilling demand from its own sales as well as demand transferred from Store A sales.

Calculating split factors

The calculation used to determine a split factor is:

# of Units sold at [Sales Location] that were fulfilled at [Fulfillment Location] /
# of Units sold at [Sales Location]

Example: Buy Online, Pickup In Store (BOPIS)

Consider an E-Commerce site which gives customers the choice of picking up items from their local store or having the item shipped to their home. In such a scenario, all sales are credited to the E-Commerce site, but fulfillment of sales will be split among multiple locations based on customer choices. If a customer chooses to pick up the item in a store, fulfillment is transferred to the pickup store. If they choose to ship it to their home, fulfilment stays at the E-Commerce location.

In this manner, split factors can be calculated for all locations.

Once split factors have been calculated from sales at all locations (E-Commerce, Store 73, Store 241, Store 405), JustEnough uses them to forecast fulfillment demand for each location.

Applying split factors

To calculate how much stock on hand is needed to handle a location’s anticipated demand, JustEnough applies the split factors calculated from each location’s sales history to each location’s sales demand forecast. For example, say JustEnough creates a sales forecast for the next 90 days (Period 2) based on sales from the previous 90 days (Period 1). To then calculate anticipated fulfillment demand at each location, JustEnough:

  • Calculates split factors for each location based on Period 1 fulfillment splits (see Examples above).
  • Applies the Period 1 split factors to the Period 2 sales forecasts (see Examples below).
  • Sums up the split factor results for each location from across all locations (see Examples below).

You can configure JustEnough to use split factors when calculating an item’s replenishment strategy. See Demand Transfer Configuration for details.
